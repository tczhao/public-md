# 08 — Questions to Ask

"Any questions for me?" is graded. It's not optional, and "no, I think you covered everything" is the worst possible answer. Strong questions signal that you've thought about the role beyond the JD, that you'd be a curious teammate, and that you're choosing them — not just hoping to be chosen.

## How many questions to prepare

Have **6–8 questions ready** per interview. You'll usually only get to ask 2–4. Prep more than you'll need so you can pick what fits the conversation.

## Tier them

Tier the questions to the interviewer:

- **Recruiter:** logistics, process, timeline, comp ranges, what they look for in strong candidates.
- **Hiring manager:** team priorities, success criteria, biggest challenges, their leadership style.
- **Future peer:** day-to-day, team dynamics, what they wish they'd known.
- **Skip-level / executive:** strategy, where the team fits in the org, the bet they're making.
- **Bar raiser / cross-functional:** how teams collaborate, what they look for in good partners.

Asking a peer about long-term company strategy is fine but underuses them. Asking the executive about onboarding cadence is a waste of an executive.

## A bank of strong questions

### About the role

- "What does great look like at the 6-month and 12-month mark for someone in this role?"
- "What's the top problem you're hoping the person in this seat will solve in their first quarter?"
- "What's the hardest part of this role that isn't obvious from the JD?"
- "How would you describe the balance between [X and Y dimensions of the role]?" (e.g., independent work vs. collaboration, depth vs. breadth)

### About the team

- "How is the team organized? Who would I be working with day-to-day?"
- "What's a recent decision the team made that you're proud of?"
- "What's something the team is still figuring out?"
- "How does the team handle disagreement on priorities or technical direction?"

### About the manager (when interviewing the manager)

- "How do you like to give feedback, and how often?"
- "What's something a previous report did that surprised you, in a good way?"
- "How do you think about career growth for someone in this role?"
- "What would I learn from working with you that I might not learn elsewhere?"

### About the company / product

- "What's the bet the company is making that you'd say is least understood from the outside?"
- "If [the product / metric] looks materially different in two years, what's most likely to have changed?"
- "Who are the customers / users you most enjoy serving, and which ones are hardest?"

### About culture and ways of working

- "How do decisions get made when reasonable people disagree?"
- "What does work-life look like during a normal week vs. a crunch week?"
- "How does feedback flow — both up and down?"
- "What's something about the team's culture that you protect intentionally?"

### Diagnostic questions (signal-rich, ask carefully)

These probe for problems and reveal a lot. Ask 1–2 max, late in the loop.

- "What's something about the company / role that's harder than the marketing makes it look?"
- "What's the most common reason people on this team have left?"
- "If I joined and one year later it didn't work out, what's the most likely reason?"

These questions get honest answers from honest interviewers and reveal red flags from evasive ones. Either way, useful.

### "Destabilizing-but-gentle" questions (the differentiator)

The questions above are good. The questions below are what differentiated candidates ask — they make the interviewer pause and *think* before answering, which means the interviewer remembers you when the debrief starts.

- "What's a belief most of your team holds that you privately disagree with?"
- "If you were starting the team from scratch today, what wouldn't you rebuild?"
- "What's the most expensive lesson the team has learned in the last year?"
- "What's a decision the team made that you're confident was right but most people think is wrong?"
- "What does this team get wrong on average that you wish someone external would call out?"

These work because they assume the interviewer has views, force them to consider their own work critically, and reward them for honesty rather than rehearsed answers. They land especially well with senior interviewers, hiring managers, and skip-levels.

A few rules:

- Ask one of these per loop, not one per interview. Save it for the senior or most engaged interviewer.
- Read the room. If the interviewer is rushed or buttoned-up, default to safer questions.
- Be ready to actually engage with the answer. Don't ask "what's a belief most of your team disagrees with" and then have nothing to say when they share one.

### Questions to *get* signal back from them

- "Based on our conversation, is there anything about my background you'd like me to expand on?"
- "Are there gaps you see in my profile compared to what you're looking for?"

The second one is brave but powerful. Often you'll get an honest answer — and a chance to address the concern in the same call.

## Questions to avoid

- Anything answered on the careers page or About page. ("What does your company do?" — auto-fail.)
- Comp questions to anyone but the recruiter. (Right time, wrong audience.)
- "Do I get to work on cool projects?" — vague, low-signal.
- "How many vacation days do I get?" — fine eventually, not in interview rounds.
- "Are you guys going to acquire / IPO / get acquired?" — they can't tell you, and asking flags you as a risk.
- "Are you hiring for any other roles?" — feels off-target. Unless you've been told the original role is closed.

## Customizing per interviewer

Before each interview, do a 10-minute prep:

- Look up the interviewer on LinkedIn. Note their tenure, team, prior companies.
- Search them on Google / their company blog. Have they written or spoken anything publicly?
- Pick one question that ties to their specific background. "I noticed you came from [prior company / role]. What surprised you most about moving to [current company]?"

Personalized questions stick. Generic questions make you forgettable.

## The follow-up move

After they answer, do one of two things:

1. **Tie it back to you.** "That tracks with what I learned in [prior project] — I found that [specific connection]." Demonstrates you can connect what they said to your experience.
2. **Ask one follow-up.** "That's interesting — when you say [X], do you mean [specific clarification]?" Shows you're listening, not reading from a list.

Avoid asking a question, getting an answer, and immediately going to the next question. That reads as scripted.

## The closing question

Before you hang up — if it makes sense — ask about next steps:

> "I really enjoyed the conversation. Could you tell me what the next steps look like and roughly when I should expect to hear back?"

Then a soft enthusiasm signal:

> "Thank you for the time — this conversation made me more excited about the role."

Be honest with the second one. If you're not more excited, don't fake it. But if you are, say it. Recruiters and hiring managers track candidate enthusiasm and it weighs in tight decisions.
