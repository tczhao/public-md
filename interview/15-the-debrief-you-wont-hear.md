# 15 — The Debrief You'll Never Hear

After your loop, your interviewers gather (in person or in a shared doc) and write up what they think of you. The hiring committee reads those write-ups. The decision is made there, not during your interview. The candidate who wins the offer is usually not the one who answered the most questions correctly — it's the one whose interviewers wrote the right *sentences* about them.

This doc reverse-engineers the playbook from the language of the debrief. If you optimize for "the sentence the interviewer will type into the hiring tool," you'll prepare differently than if you optimize for "answer the question well."

## The vocabulary that wins

These are the phrases that show up in offer-leaning debriefs across companies. Each one corresponds to a behavior you can deliberately demonstrate in a 45-minute slot.

### "High agency"

Translation: this person makes things happen without being told what to do.

How to show it:

- **Bring something they didn't ask for.** A 1-page teardown of their product, a small reproduction of their public dataset, a sketch of how you'd approach the role's first 30 days. Hand it over (printed) or share-screen it (virtual). Casual: "I had time this morning so I sketched this — happy to walk through if you're curious."
- **Steer the interview** when they leave space. "I have a couple of directions I think would be most useful — want me to walk through the project, or jump to the technical question first?"
- **Follow up with something*useful*, not just thanks.** Send a 3-paragraph extension of your in-loop discussion: "You mentioned the team's debating X. I thought about it more — here's where I land and the tradeoff I'd flag."

### "Scrappy" / "resourceful"

Translation: this person figures out hard problems with limited resources.

How to show it:

- **Tell stories where you didn't have what you needed.** Missing data, no headcount, no precedent, no budget. Be specific about the constraint and what you did anyway.
- **In technical questions, when stuck, narrate a workaround.** "I don't remember the exact syntax for X — I'd Google it in real life. The shape of the answer is…"
- **Pick projects in your portfolio that show resourcefulness over polish.** A scrappy MVP that worked beats a beautiful but routine class assignment.

### "Raises the bar" / "I'd be lucky to work with them"

Translation: this person would make my team better.

How to show it:

- **Ask the kind of question that makes the interviewer think.** Not "what does your team do?" — "what's a belief most of your team holds that you privately disagree with?" / "if you were rebuilding the team today, what wouldn't you rebuild?" / "what's the most expensive lesson the team has learned in the last year?"
- **Disagree intelligently when you do.** When the interviewer says something you have a real take on, say so — calibrated. "Interesting — my instinct would push back on that. Here's why, but I'd want to know more about your context before I'm sure."
- **Reference work outside what was asked.** A paper you read recently, a tool you tried last weekend, a podcast that connects to what they just described. *Real* references — naming a paper you didn't read is worse than naming nothing.

### "Curious" / "intellectually engaged"

Translation: this person actually cares about the work, not just the job.

How to show it:

- **Have a current obsession in your field that you can talk about for 5 minutes.** A specific problem, a specific tool, a specific debate. Not "AI" — *which* problem in AI keeps you up.
- **Follow specific people / publications / projects.** "The team that wrote the [paper / tool / blog post] is part of why I applied here." Real, specific, defensible.
- **React to what the interviewer says.** "That's interesting — that contradicts what I would have guessed from [external source]. Why does it work that way?"

### "Self-aware"

Translation: this person knows what they're good at and what they're not, and they don't bullshit.

How to show it:

- **In failure stories: name your fault clearly.** Not "the project had challenges" — "I made the wrong call on day 3."
- **Don't oversell weaknesses as strengths.** "I'm a perfectionist" reads as scripted. Real weakness with a real growth move beats it every time.
- **Calibrate confidence visibly.** "I'm fairly confident on X. Less sure on Y — I've seen it once but haven't done it end-to-end."

### "Coachable" / "would grow fast"

Translation: this person will be materially better in 6 months than they are today.

How to show it:

- **Take feedback in real time.** When the interviewer hints, *visibly update*. "Oh — you're right, the edge case I missed is X. Let me redo that."
- **In behavioral, show a moment where feedback changed your behavior.** Specific feedback → specific change → specific outcome.
- **Ask for feedback at the end.** "Is there anything in our conversation today you'd push me to work on?" Most candidates don't ask. The ones who do hear something useful and signal coachability.

### "Strong communicator"

Translation: I could put this person in front of a customer / executive / senior partner without sweating.

How to show it:

- **Translate technical depth into plain language *and back*.** Practice both directions.
- **Number your points out loud.** "Three things — first… second… third…"
- **End your answers with a clear stop.** Don't trail off. Use a closing that gives the interviewer a chance to engage: "…that's the version I'd recommend. Want me to dig into the tradeoffs or move on?"

## The sentences you do *not* want written

Common debrief phrases that translate to no-hire:

- **"Smart but I couldn't tell what they actually did."** → You said "we" too much.
- **"Knew the right answers but didn't seem like they cared."** → No visible curiosity, no opinions, no follow-ups.
- **"Solid but unmemorable."** → No specific story stuck. No vivid detail. No moment.
- **"Would need a lot of hand-holding."** → You didn't show agency. You waited for instruction.
- **"Process was off."** → You jumped to the answer without clarifying / didn't narrate / didn't verify.
- **"Couldn't handle pushback."** → You either caved instantly or got defensive.
- **"Didn't have any real questions for us."** → You didn't ask, or asked questions answered on the careers page.

## The reframe

Stop preparing for the question. Prepare for the *sentence* that gets typed about you when the interviewer fills in the form 5 minutes after you leave.

Before any major loop, write down the 1–2 sentences you most want each interviewer to write. Reverse from there:

- "I want the technical interviewer to write: *'demonstrated agency by bringing a small teardown of our product to the call; calibrated about what they don't know; coachable in real time.'*"
- "I want the behavioral interviewer to write: *'specific stories with real numbers; named their own mistakes without apology; one memorable moment about the timestamp bug at 9pm Friday.'*"

Then make sure those behaviors actually show up in the conversation. This is the unit of preparation that the rest of this playbook builds toward.

## A short checklist before any major interview

- One artifact you can casually share that they didn't ask for.
- One opinion you can defend on a topic relevant to their work.
- One "destabilizing-but-gentle" question for each interviewer.
- One specific external reference (paper, tool, person, project) you can connect to.
- One genuinely-self-aware weakness with a real growth move.
- One vivid story-detail you'll sneak into the loop somewhere.
- A plan to ask for feedback at the end.

Hit five of seven and your debrief sentences write themselves.
