# 13 — Non-Traditional Paths

The rest of the playbook implicitly assumes a US-citizen, on-time-graduating, full-time-degree candidate with no constraints. That's a narrow audience. This doc covers the angles for everyone else: international students, career changers, bootcamp grads, late grads, returners, and candidates needing accommodations.

## International students (US-focused, principles apply elsewhere)

### What you need to know up front

- **Most US new-grad roles require visa sponsorship eventually** (H-1B), even when you're starting on OPT.
- **Some companies don't sponsor.** This isn't always advertised. Filter early.
- **STEM OPT extension** (24 months on top of standard 12) is a meaningful tailwind for STEM-designated programs. Make sure your degree CIP code qualifies.
- **The H-1B lottery** is real. Companies that hire many internationals usually plan for it. Smaller companies often won't take the risk.

### Filtering companies

Before applying, check sponsorship history on:

- **MyVisaJobs** — searchable LCA database.
- **H1bdata.info** — searchable salary data by company and role.
- **Levels.fyi visa filter.**

Rough heuristic: if a company hasn't sponsored ≥10 H-1Bs in the last 2 years, the role you're looking at probably doesn't sponsor either. Don't waste cycles.

### Disclosure timing

When asked "are you authorized to work in the US?" — answer truthfully. The follow-up question is "will you require sponsorship now or in the future?" — also answer truthfully.

- Saying "I don't need sponsorship now, but will in 1–2 years" is normal for OPT students. Don't lie.
- If a recruiter says "we don't sponsor," thank them and move on. The role isn't for you.
- If you're unsure of company policy, ask the recruiter early — usually first call. Saves everyone time.

### Cover letters and interviews

You don't need to mention visa status until asked. Don't lead with it. Lead with the role and your fit, exactly like any other candidate.

When it does come up, frame neutrally:

> "I'm authorized to work on F-1 OPT for [12 / 36] months from graduation, and I'd require H-1B sponsorship after that. I understand sponsorship is a real cost and I want to be upfront about it — let me know if it changes anything in the process."

### Network among returnees and grads who got sponsored

The single most useful conversations: international students from your school 1–2 years ahead who got sponsored at the companies you're targeting. They know who actually sponsors at the new-grad level and which managers fight for it.

## Career changers and late-career new grads

If you're 28+, switching from another field, with a fresh degree or bootcamp:

- **Lead with the transition story, not "I'm starting over."** "I spent 5 years in [prior field], and on [specific project] I started doing [adjacent work] which led me to retrain in [new field]. Here's what I bring that a 22-year-old doesn't…"
- **Translate prior experience into target-role language.** A teacher pivoting to UX research has stakeholder management, user empathy, observational skills, structured curriculum design — name them.
- **Don't apologize for age or prior career.** It's an asset. You've shown you can deliver in a job, manage a budget, communicate with adults.
- **The "why now / why this" question gets harder.** Have a real answer. "I worked with [tool / data / process / customer] in my last role and realized I cared more about [adjacent thing] than [original thing]." Specific, not vague.

## Bootcamp grads (especially in tech / data)

Companies vary wildly in how they treat bootcamp grads:

- **Pro-bootcamp:** many startups, some growth-stage companies, agencies, contract roles.
- **Bootcamp-skeptical:** some FAANG-tier companies, some legacy enterprises with HR-locked degree requirements.
- **Indifferent if your skills are visible:** most companies — if you ship a portfolio that proves the skill, the bootcamp is a footnote.

Your portfolio is doing the heaviest lifting. Three things that compound:

1. **One real project that doesn't look like a tutorial.** Not "Twitter clone." Something with a real user, real data, or a real problem.
2. **Public writing about your learning.** Blog posts, LinkedIn articles, a GitHub README that walks through your reasoning.
3. **Contributions to open source or community.** Even small. Demonstrates you can navigate other people's code.

In interviews, mention the bootcamp once as context, then pivot to artifacts. "After bootcamp I built X — let me walk you through what I learned" beats defending the bootcamp itself.

## Returners (parents, caregivers, after a gap)

If there's a gap of 1+ years:

- **Address it briefly in the cover letter** if helpful, or in the first call. Don't bury it for them to find.
- **Frame the gap as time spent on something legible.** Caregiving is legit. So is health recovery, study, travel-with-purpose.
- **Show you've kept current** during the gap with a project, course, certification, or freelance work. Even one is enough to flip the narrative from "stale" to "self-directed."
- **Look for "returnship" programs** — Apple, Goldman, IBM, and others run formal return-to-work programs that are explicitly designed for this path.

## Candidates needing accommodations

If you have a disability, neurodivergence, or chronic condition that affects how you interview:

- **You don't have to disclose** unless you need an accommodation.
- **If you do need one, ask early.** "Could I have 50% additional time on the technical assessment?" or "Could the live coding round be done in a written format?" These are routine asks. ADA-covered employers must engage.
- **Email is the better channel** than verbal — creates a paper trail.
- **You don't owe a medical explanation.** "I have a documented disability and am requesting [specific accommodation]" is sufficient.
- **Recruiters are usually allies here.** Most companies have an internal accommodations process. Ask the recruiter how it works.

If the company resists or makes you feel unwelcome for asking — that's data. You probably don't want to work there.

## First-gen and underrepresented candidates

A few practical asymmetries to know about:

- **The "hidden curriculum" is real.** Things like "always send a thank-you email," "negotiate the offer," "do informational interviews" — these aren't taught in school. The whole playbook is partly an attempt to surface them.
- **Affinity networks help.** Many companies have employee resource groups (ERGs) for first-gen, women in tech, Black professionals, Latinx, LGBTQ+, etc. Many ERGs do candidate-facing events and informal referrals.
- **External fellowships and pipelines** — Management Leadership for Tomorrow, Code2040, Out for Undergrad, T. Howard Foundation, and many more. Worth applying to as parallel paths.
- **Don't filter yourself out.** A common pattern: women and underrepresented candidates self-screen out of roles where they only meet 70% of the listed qualifications. Apply anyway. JD lists are wishlists, not requirements.

## Industry timing (quick reference)

Different industries recruit on wildly different cycles. Approximate windows for the US:

| Industry | Start of new-grad recruiting | Most offers out |
|---|---|---|
| Investment banking | Aug–Sep (sophomore year for SA, year before for FT) | Sep–Oct |
| Management consulting | Aug–Oct | Oct–Dec |
| Big-tech engineering | Aug–Oct | Oct–Feb |
| Quant / trading | Jul–Sep | Aug–Oct |
| Big-tech non-engineering (PM, data) | Sep–Dec | Oct–Mar |
| Startups / mid-size tech | Year-round, peaks Jan–Apr | Year-round |
| Big 4 / accounting | Aug–Sep | Sep–Nov |
| CPG / consumer brands | Sep–Jan | Nov–Mar |
| Marketing agencies | Feb–Apr | Mar–May |
| Government / public sector | Year-round, slow | Variable, often slow |
| Healthcare / pharma | Sep–Mar | Variable |
| Nonprofit | Year-round | Often spring/summer |
| Media / publishing | Spring/summer | Summer/fall |

If you're targeting tech and your school's career fair is in April, you're already late to most fall-recruiting tracks. Adjust accordingly.

## The meta-lesson

The default "new grad" framing in most career-services material is narrow. If you don't fit it, the playbook still works — but you'll need to think a layer deeper about timing, sponsorship, narrative framing, and which companies are realistic. This isn't a barrier; it's information. Use it.
