# 00 — Mindset and Preparation

Before you write a resume, read a job description, or send a single LinkedIn message, get the meta-game right. New grads who treat the search like an essay assignment ("apply to 200 places, hope") underperform new grads who treat it like a 12-week project with a roadmap.

## Reframe the search

- **You are not asking for a favor.** Hiring managers have an open headcount because they need a problem solved. Your job is to convince them you'll solve it. That is a transaction, not a plea.
- **Rejection is signal, not verdict.** A "no" usually means "wrong fit, wrong time, or wrong story." Almost never "you are bad." Treat each one as data.
- **The market is illegible.** Two equally qualified candidates can get wildly different outcomes. Don't over-fit to any single rejection or success.

## Set up the project

Create a tracking spreadsheet (Notion, Airtable, or Google Sheets) with columns:

| Company | Role | Source | Date applied | Referral? | Stage | Next action | Date of next action | Salary band | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Stages: `Applied → Recruiter screen → HM screen → Loop → Team match → Offer → Closed`.

Review the sheet every Sunday for 30 minutes. Anything stuck for >7 days needs a follow-up or a write-off.

## Time budget (per week)

For an active search:

- **Networking & referrals:** 6–8 hours (highest ROI, almost everyone underinvests).
- **Targeted applications:** 3–4 hours (10–15 carefully tailored apps > 200 spray-and-pray).
- **Interview prep:** 4–6 hours (story bank, technical drills, case practice).
- **Skill building:** 2–3 hours (one project or course tied to your target role).
- **Recovery:** non-negotiable. The search is months long, not weeks.

## Build a story bank early

Before you have a single interview scheduled, write down 8–12 stories from school, internships, projects, clubs, jobs. For each:

- 1-line title (so you can find it under pressure).
- Situation, Task, Action, Result (STAR).
- 3–5 "tags" for the signal it shows: leadership, conflict, ambiguity, technical depth, stakeholder mgmt, failure, initiative.

You will reuse these stories across 20+ interviews. Investing in them once is the highest-leverage prep activity after networking.

## Target list, not a wish list

Build a list of 30–50 companies you'd genuinely take an offer from. For each, note:

- 1–2 specific roles you'd apply to.
- Why you (specifically) fit (this becomes the cover letter / referral message hook).
- Anyone you know there, or 1 alum you'll reach out to.

Then prioritize: tier-1 dream (5), tier-2 strong (15), tier-3 practice/safe (10–30).

**Sequencing — the part most playbooks get wrong.** Tier-1 dream postings can fill in the first 1–2 weeks. You cannot afford to "wait until you're warmed up" for them. Apply to tier-1 the moment a fitting role opens, *and* keep a parallel tier-2/tier-3 pipeline running so your interviewing reps build at the same time. Most tier-1 loops happen 4–8 weeks after application — that's plenty of warmup if you're actively working tier-2/tier-3 in the meantime.

The mistake is "I'll polish for two months then apply to my dream company." By month two the role is closed. Apply now, prep in parallel.

## What "ready" looks like before you start applying

- Resume passes the 6-second skim test (see doc 01).
- LinkedIn headline and summary updated (see doc 02).
- Story bank has at least 8 stories tagged.
- You can articulate, in two sentences, why this type of role and not another.
- You have a tracking sheet open and one application as a dry run.

## Mental health, candidly

You will be ghosted. You will be rejected after a 6-hour loop. You will see classmates announce offers on LinkedIn while you have nothing. None of that is signal about your worth. Three concrete habits that matter:

- **Decouple identity from outcome.** "I got rejected" not "I am a reject."
- **Process over results.** Track activities (apps, conversations, mock interviews), not just offers.
- **Have one non-search anchor.** A sport, a hobby, a friend group that doesn't ask "any updates?" every week.

## Common new-grad framing mistakes

- "I'll start applying once I'm ready." You become ready by applying. Apply to safe-tier roles in week 1.
- "I don't have anything impressive to talk about." You do — you just haven't structured it. See doc 04.
- "Networking feels gross." Reframe: you're asking for a 20-minute conversation about their job, not a favor. Most people say yes.
- "I'll just take whatever I can get." Fine as a fallback, but you'll negotiate worse and be likelier to leave in a year. Have a target.
