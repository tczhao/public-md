# 10 — Follow-up and Offers

The work between interviews matters as much as the interviews themselves. Most candidates lose ground in the gaps — silent, anxious, scattered. The candidates who run a tight follow-up game compound small advantages: faster timelines, better recruiter rapport, cleaner offer comparisons.

## After every interview

Within 24 hours, do three things:

1. **Send a thank-you email.** To every interviewer you talked to, individually if possible.
2. **Journal the interview** in your tracking sheet: what was asked, what went well, what fumbled, what to add to your story bank.
3. **Update your tracking sheet status** to the next stage and the expected next-action date.

### The thank-you email

Short. Specific. Sincere.

> Hi [Name],
>
> Thank you for the time today. [One specific thing from the conversation that landed for you — a comment they made, a project you discussed, a question they asked.]
>
> [One sentence tying back to fit — what you walked away more excited about, or one quick clarification on something you fumbled.]
>
> Looking forward to next steps.
>
> Best,
> [Your name]

If you blanked on a technical question, this is your chance to recover:

> "You asked about [X]. After the conversation I worked through it more carefully — the approach I'd take is [one short paragraph]. Wanted to share since I felt like I didn't give it justice in the moment."

This works *if* the answer is genuinely good. It does not save a fundamentally weak performance.

## Following up when they go silent

Recruiters disappear. Sometimes for legit reasons (loop scheduling, headcount holds, holidays), sometimes because of process gaps. You have to follow up.

### Cadence

- **Day 0:** post-interview thank-you.
- **Day 5–7 of silence:** first nudge to the recruiter.
- **Day 12–14:** second nudge.
- **Day 20+:** consider it cold. One last nudge with a soft signal that you're moving forward elsewhere if true.

### Sample first nudge

> Hi [Recruiter],
>
> Hope your week is going well. Just following up on next steps for the [role] interview I had on [date]. Let me know if there's anything you need from me on my end. Still excited about the team.
>
> Best,
> [Name]

### Sample second nudge

> Hi [Recruiter],
>
> Wanted to gently re-surface this — I had my [final round] for [role] on [date] and want to make sure I'm staying on your radar. Happy to jump on a quick call if there's anything I can address. If timing or process has shifted, totally understand — appreciate any update you can share.
>
> Best,
> [Name]

### When you have a competing deadline

If another offer is forcing your hand, share it directly:

> Hi [Recruiter], wanted to flag — I have an offer from [company / "another company"] with a deadline of [date]. [Company] is my top choice and I'd love to bring my decision together. Is there any chance we can accelerate the [next-step / final decision] so I can decide with full information?

Recruiters can often pull schedules forward by 1–2 weeks for a real exploding offer.

## Managing multiple processes

Goal: cluster offer dates within a 2-week window so you can compare. To do this, you need to start your search funnel-conscious.

- **Top-of-funnel (apps + first calls):** spread out so you have ~5 in flight per week.
- **Mid-funnel (loops):** try to schedule loops in a tight window — 2–3 in the same 2 weeks.
- **End of funnel (offers):** if one company is moving fast, ask the others to accelerate.

This is normal and recruiters do it routinely on their side. You're not being aggressive — you're managing a process.

## Receiving an offer (full sequence)

1. **Verbal:** "Thank you so much. I'm really excited. Could you send the details in writing? I'll review and come back with any questions within [timeframe]."
2. **Within 24 hours:** confirm in writing you have the offer, restate the deadline, ask any clarifying questions about components.
3. **Within 48 hours:** notify other companies you're in process with that you have a competing offer.
4. **Within deadline:** decide and respond.

### Notifying other companies

Email to other recruiters where you're still active:

> Hi [Name],
>
> Wanted to keep you in the loop. I've received an offer from [company / "another company in the space"] with a deadline of [date]. [Company you're emailing] is genuinely a top choice for me and I'd love to make my decision with full information. Is there flexibility to accelerate the [next step] before [deadline]?
>
> Happy to do whatever I can to make timing work.
>
> Best,
> [Name]

This usually works. If it doesn't, sometimes the right call is to let the slower company go — better than scrambling on a 24-hour decision with bad info.

## Comparing offers

Don't compare base salaries head-to-head. Use a structured matrix:

| Factor | Company A | Company B | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total year-1 comp (base + sign-on + vested equity) | | | |
| 4-year total comp (if equity is meaningful) | | | |
| Role / scope / first-year work | | | |
| Manager fit (gut check) | | | |
| Team / peers | | | |
| Learning trajectory next 2 years | | | |
| Brand / network it gives me | | | |
| Location / remote setup | | | |
| Risk (company stage, runway, layoffs) | | | |
| Gut feeling | | | |

Score each 1–5 if it helps. The goal isn't to mechanize the decision — it's to surface what you're actually weighting.

For most new grads:

- **Manager > company brand > comp** in long-run impact, *if* the comp is in a reasonable range.
- **Comp gaps below ~10% are usually noise** at this career stage — fit and learning matter more.
- **Comp gaps above ~25% deserve weight** but rarely justify joining a clearly worse role/team.

## Declining an offer (well)

Be gracious and brief. Don't burn bridges. The recruiting world is small.

> Hi [Recruiter],
>
> Thank you so much for the offer and for the time you and the team invested in me. After a lot of consideration, I've decided to accept another opportunity that aligns more closely with [specific reason — role focus, location, etc.].
>
> [Company] genuinely impressed me and I appreciated [specific detail]. I hope our paths cross again.
>
> Best,
> [Name]

Don't badmouth the company. Don't tell them which competitor you took. Don't disappear without a reply.

## Renegotiating doesn't end at "accepted"

Once you've signed:

- **Update LinkedIn** (after the official start date or as agreed).
- **Send the recruiter a thank-you** plus a small note 1 month in. Recruiters move teams. Future you wants the relationship.
- **Stay in touch with people you interviewed with** who weren't your hiring manager. Future allies internally.

## If you're rejected

After the sting wears off, send a short note:

> Hi [Name],
>
> Thank you for letting me know and for the time you invested in me through the process. I learned a lot from the conversations — especially [one specific detail]. If you ever have feedback you'd be willing to share, I'd genuinely value it. And I'd love to stay in touch in case opportunities open up in the future.
>
> Best,
> [Name]

Some recruiters share feedback. Some don't. Either way, you've left the door open. Many candidates get hired by the same company 6–18 months later, often into a better role.

## Withdrawing from a process

If your priorities shift mid-loop, withdraw cleanly. Don't ghost.

> Hi [Name], I wanted to let you know that I'm withdrawing from the process. After more reflection on what I'm looking for, I don't think this role is the right fit at this stage. I really appreciated the conversations and the time the team gave me.

Take 1 minute. Pays dividends.
