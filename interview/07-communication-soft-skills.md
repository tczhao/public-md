# 07 — Communication and Soft Skills

When two candidates have similar technical skill, communication decides the offer. For new grads especially — where technical depth is by definition limited — communication is often the largest swing factor in the loop.

## What "good communication" actually looks like

Three observable behaviors that interviewers reliably score on:

1. **First-attempt clarity.** They understood you the first time, without follow-up clarification.
2. **Audience adjustment.** You explain technical detail to a non-technical interviewer differently than to a technical one.
3. **Two-way fluency.** You ask questions that move the conversation forward, and you listen to the answers.

## Lead with a position, not a hedge

When asked a question, lead with the answer in one sentence, then justify. This is sometimes called BLUF (bottom line up front) or the inverted pyramid.

Bad: "Well, in my last project we had this dataset, and originally I thought we'd use a linear regression, but then…"
Good: "I'd recommend a logistic regression. The target is binary and we need interpretable coefficients. Here's why I considered and rejected the alternatives…"

This applies to behavioral, technical, and case answers. Lead with the conclusion. Trust the interviewer to ask for the path if they want it.

### Calibrated conviction beats hedged correctness

A common new-grad failure mode: answering correctly but with so much hedging that the interviewer can't tell what you actually think. "It depends" / "I could see both sides" / "there's no right answer" — said in isolation — sounds careful but reads as no opinion.

Top firms hire for judgment, which requires visible opinions. The move is to *take a position and name what would change your mind*:

- Hedge: "Both A and B have tradeoffs, it really depends on the context."
- Position: "I'd pick A. The two reasons are [X] and [Y]. The case for B I'd take seriously is [specific scenario] — if I learned that was the situation, I'd switch."

You're not committing to the answer for life. You're showing you can hold an opinion and still be open. That's exactly the signal senior interviewers grade on.

## Speak in chunks of three

When you have multiple points, *number them out loud*: "Three reasons. First… Second… Third…"

This does three things:

- Forces you to structure before you talk.
- Lets the interviewer follow and take notes.
- Tells them when you're done, so they know they can move on.

Even when you only have two points, "two reasons" is better than a meandering list.

## Pace, pause, and silence

- **Slow down by ~20% from your natural pace.** Nervous candidates rush. Slower = more confident.
- **Pause before you answer.** 2–3 seconds is fine. Buys thinking time and signals deliberateness.
- **Use silence as punctuation.** After an important point, stop. Don't trail off into "yeah so anyways…"
- **Don't fill silences they leave.** If they pause, they're often taking notes or thinking. Wait. Candidates who keep talking through interviewer silence often dig holes.

## Filler words

The four to kill: "um," "uh," "like," "you know."

You won't eliminate them entirely. But cutting from 1 every 5 seconds to 1 every 30 seconds noticeably raises perceived competence.

The exercise: record yourself answering 3 questions. Count fillers. Re-record. Count again. The awareness alone cuts them by 50%.

## Active listening (the underrated skill)

- **Confirm before answering** if the question is complex. "Just to make sure I'm understanding, you're asking about X — is that right?"
- **Reference what they said** when you respond. "You mentioned the team uses A — given that, I'd…"
- **Don't pre-build your answer while they're still talking.** You'll miss the actual question. Listen all the way through.

## Body language (video and in-person)

- **Eye contact:** look at the camera lens, not at the small video of yourself. For in-person, look at the interviewer's eyes ~60% of the time, not 100%.
- **Posture:** sit slightly forward, not slouched. Open posture signals engagement.
- **Hands:** visible if possible, but not covering your face. Resist tapping or clicking.
- **Smile occasionally.** Especially on the warm-up and the close. Resting interview face reads as cold or anxious.
- **Nod at appropriate times** while they're talking. Signals you're tracking.

## Story craft (the differentiator)

STAR is the floor. The candidates who win competing offers tell stories that get *quoted in the debrief*. Three moves separate them.

### 1. Open with stakes or dialogue, not setting

Generic: "So in my internship last summer, our team was working on a dashboard…"

Differentiated: "My PM messaged me at 9pm Friday: 'Marketing leadership is presenting this Monday and the numbers don't match.' I had the weekend to figure out what was wrong."

Same situation, completely different memorability.

### 2. The vivid noun

Each story should have *one specific detail* that makes it stick. A quote, a number, an unusual constraint, a name. The detail is what the interviewer remembers when they're filling in the hiring tool 5 minutes after you leave.

- Forgettable: "I found a data quality issue and fixed it."
- Memorable: "On day 3 I realized 30% of records had bad timestamps from a DST bug — three different timezones merged into one column."

Pick the detail deliberately. It should be something only your story has. "The 30% timestamp bug" becomes shorthand for you.

### 3. The portable closer

End each story with a one-liner the interviewer can repeat. "That's the project where I learned to audit the data before trusting it — I've done it on every project since." Or after a failure: "One sentence: measure the data before you trust it."

The closer compresses your story into something an interviewer can carry into the debrief room.

### Other small moves

- **Use specific names and numbers.** "Sarah, the marketing lead" beats "the marketing person." "$40K of wasted spend" beats "a lot of waste."
- **Show, don't claim.** "I led the project" → tell me the moment you led. "I'm collaborative" → tell me the conversation that proves it.

## Translating technical to non-technical

Recruiters and many hiring partners are not deeply technical. Test yourself: can you explain your top project to your grandparent in 60 seconds? If not, practice until you can.

Layered explanation:

- **One sentence:** "I built a tool that flagged transactions that looked suspicious."
- **One paragraph:** "It used historical fraud labels to learn patterns — for example, transactions far from a customer's usual location at unusual times. When it saw a similar pattern, it flagged the transaction for review. We tested it on 6 months of held-out data and it caught 30% more fraud than the existing rules."
- **Technical depth (when asked):** "It's a gradient-boosted tree on 40 engineered features, optimized for recall at a fixed precision threshold of 0.9…"

Practice all three for each project. Switch on demand.

## Reading the room

- **If they keep checking the time:** wrap up faster, get to the answer.
- **If they're nodding and writing:** keep going, they're absorbing.
- **If they jump in with a follow-up before you finish:** they got what they need. Stop, don't keep talking over them.
- **If their face is blank:** something didn't land. Pause and offer: "Want me to take a different angle on that?"

## Handling questions you don't know

The honest move beats the bluff. Two acceptable patterns:

1. "I haven't worked with X directly. From what I understand it does Y — happy to reason from there or move on, whichever helps."
2. "I don't know off the top of my head. My instinct would be to start with Z and figure it out from there."

Bluffing gets caught and is often the moment a candidate loses an offer. Honest "I don't know, here's how I'd find out" is a positive signal.

## Closing the interview well

When they say "any questions?" — see doc 08. Then before you sign off:

- Thank them by name.
- Say something specific you took from the conversation. ("The way you described the team's roadmap really resonated.")
- Ask about next steps and timeline if you don't already have them.
- End on energy. "Looking forward to hearing back" beats "okay bye."

## Over the long haul

Communication is a skill that improves with reps. Three habits that compound:

- **One mock per week** even when not actively interviewing. Use friends, classmates, alumni.
- **Record yourself.** It's painful and it works.
- **Read your writing out loud.** Cover letters, emails, project summaries. If it doesn't read like a person, rewrite it.
