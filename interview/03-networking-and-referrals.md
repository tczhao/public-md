# 03 — Networking and Referrals

The single highest-leverage activity in your search. A meaningful fraction of new-grad offers — internal company surveys at large tech firms have reported anywhere from ~30% to ~50%, smaller companies often higher — come through some form of referral or warm introduction. The exact percentage doesn't matter. The directional truth does: if you don't network, you're playing the search on hard mode.

## Why referrals work

A referred candidate's resume goes to a real human (often the hiring manager directly), bypasses some of the ATS noise, and arrives with a small implicit endorsement. The screen-rate uplift for referred candidates is large — often several multiples of cold applicants — though again, exact numbers vary.

This is not unfair — it's how almost every team hires when given a choice. Internalize it and use it.

## The "warm circle" first

Before cold outreach, work the people who already know you:

- **Family + family friends.** Ask: "Do you know anyone who works in [data / product / consulting / marketing]?" People underestimate this network.
- **Professors.** Specifically the 2–3 you have a real relationship with. They know alumni at companies they don't advertise.
- **TAs and grad students.** Often have industry contacts and are flattered to be asked.
- **Older students from your major.** Class ahead of you is gold. They just went through this.
- **Internship alumni.** Anyone you worked with previously, even briefly.
- **Clubs, sports teams, religious communities.** Soft ties matter.

Make a list of every name. Don't filter for "important" people — filter for "people who like me."

## The reach-out: 3 templates

### Template A — Warm contact (someone you actually know)

Subject: `Quick question about [their company / field]`

> Hi [Name],
>
> Hope you're doing well — [one specific personal touch: "great catching up at Thanksgiving" / "loved your last newsletter"].
>
> I'm [year, school, focus] and applying to [type of role] full-time. I've been particularly drawn to [their company / field] because [one specific reason]. Would you be open to a 20-minute chat about your experience there? I'd love to hear how you ended up in that role and what you'd recommend for someone trying to break in.
>
> No expectation of a referral — just learning. If you're slammed, no worries at all.
>
> Best,
> [Name]

### Template B — Alum / 2nd-degree (LinkedIn)

> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm a [year] at [school] (your alma mater) and exploring [field]. Your path from [previous role] to [current role at company] really resonated with what I'm trying to do. Would you be open to a quick 20-min call sometime in the next 2 weeks? I'd love to hear how you'd approach a search for someone in my position.
>
> Thanks for considering!
> [Name]

### Template C — Cold (someone you've never met)

> Hi [Name],
>
> I came across your profile while researching [team / company] and your work on [specific project, post, or talk] stood out — especially [one specific detail showing you actually looked]. I'm a [year, school] interested in [role] and would value 15–20 minutes of your time to learn how you think about [field / specific topic]. I know you're busy, so happy to send specific questions in advance and keep it short.
>
> Thanks either way,
> [Name]

#### What "specific reference" actually looks like

This is the part most cold messages fake. Be honest about what you've actually read or watched.

**Bad-specific (sounds like everyone else):** "I really admire your career path." / "Your post about leadership resonated with me." / "Your work in the data space is impressive."

**Real-specific (proof you looked):**

> "I came across your talk at the Tableau conference last year on retention dashboards — the bit at minute 18 about choosing weekly vs. cumulative views was something I hadn't thought about and it changed how I'm framing a project I'm working on."

> "I noticed on your GitHub you've been contributing to dbt-utils — the macro you opened last month for the surrogate key edge case is exactly the kind of thing I want to be working on."

> "Your team's Q3 case study on the SMB segment was on the careers page. The decision to deprioritize the broader top-of-funnel campaign in favor of vertical-specific landing pages is the kind of tradeoff I want to learn how to make."

If you can't find anything that specific, two options: pick a different person, or say honestly "I don't know your work directly, but I'm reaching out because [shared school / mutual contact / company team] and would value any time you can spare." Honest beats fake-specific every time.

### What if you don't have a "warm circle" in your target field?

Most new grads don't. That's fine — your warm circle is your starting node, not your end state. The expansion path:

1. **One-step-removed warm contacts.** Your mom's friend in insurance probably knows someone in finance. Your professor in economics probably has a former student in consulting. Ask the warm contact for the introduction, not the job.
2. **Alumni database.** Most schools have one. Filter by company and role. Send template B. Shared school is a real warm signal even if you've never met.
3. **Cohort 1–2 years ahead.** They went through this and have a fresh network in your target companies. They're flattered to be asked and will introduce you generously if you're easy to help.
4. **Public-facing people in your field.** Podcast guests, blog authors, conference speakers, niche newsletter writers. They've already chosen to engage publicly and reply more than you'd expect.

Build the warm circle as you go. Each conversation produces 1–2 referrals to the next. After 4–6 weeks of consistent outreach, the "no warm contacts in my field" problem is gone.

### Rules across all three

- **Specific, not generic.** Mention something only this person would notice.
- **Short.** ≤120 words. Long messages don't get read.
- **Make the ask trivial.** 15–20 minutes, you'll send questions in advance.
- **No attachments on the first message.** No resume yet. They didn't ask.
- **Don't ask for a referral on the first message.** It poisons the conversation. The referral comes later, or doesn't.

## Conversion rates (calibrate expectations)

- Warm contacts: 70–90% reply.
- Alumni / shared school or affiliation: 30–50% reply.
- Cold no shared connection: 10–20% reply.

Aim for 15–25 outbound messages a week. Expect 5–10 conversations. Of those, 1–3 will lead to referrals or strong intros over a few months.

## The conversation itself

20 minutes. They're doing you a favor. Drive it.

### First 2 minutes
"Thanks so much for the time. To make this easy for you, here's the format I had in mind: I'd love to hear how you ended up in your role, what your day-to-day looks like, and any advice you'd give someone trying to break in. Sound good?"

### Middle 15 minutes
Ask 5–7 questions. Listen more than talk. Take notes (let them know you're taking notes).

Strong questions:

- "What's a misconception people have about your role?"
- "What does a great new hire on your team do in their first 90 days?"
- "What's the most underrated skill for someone trying to do what you do?"
- "What do you wish you had known when you were in my shoes?"
- "Looking at the kinds of roles I'm targeting, what stands out as a gap I should close?"
- "Who else would you recommend I talk to?" (the multiplier question — always ask)

### Last 3 minutes
"This was incredibly helpful. Two quick things: would you be open to a follow-up note in a few weeks once I've moved on some of this advice? And — only if it feels appropriate — is there anyone else you'd recommend I reach out to?"

Then, *only* if the conversation went well and they seem genuinely engaged: "If a role at [their company] opened up that fit my background, would you be open to me reaching out before applying?" Soft, optional, easy to decline.

### Within 24 hours
Send a thank-you note. 3 sentences. Reference one specific thing they said. Mention one action you're going to take based on it.

### Within 4 weeks
Follow up with an update. "You suggested I work on X — here's what I built / who I talked to / what I learned." This is the part 95% of people skip and is the actual unlock.

## When to ask for the referral

After the conversation, after the follow-up, when a real role opens up. Message:

> Hi [Name], hope your [thing they mentioned] is going well. A role just opened up on the [team] team — link here. Based on our chat I think it's a strong fit for the reasons we discussed (X and Y). Would you be willing to refer me? I've attached my resume. Totally understand if not — figured I'd ask directly rather than make you guess.

Make it easy for them to say yes. Most companies have a referral portal — many people will say "send me your resume and the JD link" and submit it the same day if you make it frictionless.

## Career fairs and on-campus

If your school has them, treat them as triage:

- **Before:** research the 5–10 companies you care most about. Bring questions specific to each.
- **During:** target those 5–10 first. Skip the long lines for booths you don't care about. Get the recruiter's name and confirm the next step.
- **After (within 24 hours):** LinkedIn invite + 3-line message referencing the conversation, plus apply through their portal.

Career fairs are not for collecting swag. They're for converting a recruiter's attention into a follow-up email.

## Coffee chats with classmates and ahead-cohort

Free, easy, and most students don't do enough of them. Two coffee chats a week with people 1–3 years ahead of you in your target field will reshape your search. They know which companies are actually good, which interviewers ask what, and which recruiters are responsive.

## Following up that actually compounds

Most networks are one-conversation-and-die. Someone gives you 25 minutes, you send a thank-you note, and that's the last they ever hear from you. The candidates who land — and who keep landing for the next decade — treat the network as a long-running asset, not a single transaction. The mechanism is dead simple: stay in touch, with substance, on a schedule.

### The 4-week check-in

Four weeks after the conversation, send an update. Not "just checking in" — a real, specific update on something they suggested.

Worked example. You spoke with a senior analyst at a fintech. She told you to (1) build a small dashboard project using public data and (2) read a specific book on experimentation.

> Hi Maya,
>
> Following up from our chat in early March. You suggested I build something with public data instead of just listing coursework — I built a dashboard on NYC subway ridership recovery post-COVID using the MTA open data, and ended up writing a short post on the segmentation surprises (link). It's already come up in two interviews.
>
> I also got through *Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments* — the chapter on novelty effects reframed how I'm thinking about a take-home I'm working on for [Company].
>
> No ask — just wanted to close the loop because your advice actually changed what I did. Hope the [team offsite she mentioned] went well.
>
> [Name]

This works because it's specific, it shows you took the advice seriously, and it has no ask. The "you told me to do X — I did, here's what happened" structure is the whole game. People remember candidates who execute on their advice.

### The 3-month update

Three months in, send a longer update on where the search landed. If you got an offer at a company they helped you reach, say so explicitly and credit them. If you're still searching, share what you've learned and what's working. Keep it under 200 words.

> Hi Maya — quick update. I accepted an offer at [Company] starting in July. The conversation with you was one of three that genuinely shifted my approach (specifically the bit about not pitching myself as a generalist). Wanted you to know it mattered. Would love to stay in touch — and if there's ever a way I can help, even just connecting you with a student in my program, please ask.

### The 6-month touchpoint

Six months after starting, send a one-paragraph note on how the role is going, what surprised you, and one specific thing you learned that connects back to something they said. This is the move that converts a "person who once helped me" into a peer relationship that lasts a career.

Three follow-ups over six months. That's it. Almost nobody does it, which is exactly why it works.

## Common mistakes

- Spamming "I'm interested in opportunities at your company" to 100 people. Almost zero conversion and burns bridges.
- Asking for a referral on first contact. Burns trust.
- Going silent after they helped. Always close the loop.
- Talking more than listening. They're not interviewing you (yet) — they're sharing.
- Treating the network as transactional. People can tell. Be genuinely curious about their work.
