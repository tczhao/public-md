# 09 — Salary Negotiation

New grads leave more money on the table than any other cohort. Half of them never negotiate at all. Of those who do, many ask once, get a small bump, and stop. This is the highest hourly-rate work you will do all year — a 30-minute conversation can move your offer by $5K–$30K, plus signing bonus and equity. Take it seriously.

## The myths to retire

- "If I negotiate, they'll rescind." Almost never happens. Companies expect negotiation. A confident, polite ask does not poison anything.
- "They'll think I'm greedy." They've negotiated their own offers. They know how this works.
- "I should be grateful for any offer." Be grateful, *and* negotiate. Not mutually exclusive.
- "There's no room — it's a fixed band." There is almost always some flexibility, especially across the comp components (base, sign-on, equity, relocation, start date).

## Before any number is discussed

Two principles, drilled in:

1. **Don't be the first to name a number** if you can avoid it. The first number anchors the conversation.
2. **Have data before the conversation.** Know the market range for the role, level, and location.

Sources for new-grad comp data:

- **Levels.fyi** — best for tech roles, has new-grad data.
- **Glassdoor / Salary.com / Payscale** — broader, less precise.
- **Blind** (anonymous) — useful for tech and adjacent.
- **Repvue** — sales-specific.
- **Your school's career center.** They often have median offer data by major and employer.
- **People in your network 1–2 years ahead.** Direct ask: "I know it's awkward — would you be willing to share a range for new-grad offers at [company]?" Many will.

For each target company / role, write down:

- **Base range** (low / median / high).
- **Sign-on range.**
- **Equity range or annual cash bonus.**
- **Total first-year comp** (base + sign-on + equity vested year 1).

Keep this in your tracking sheet.

## When the recruiter asks "what are your expectations?"

This question often comes early. Three responses, ranked by quality:

1. **Best — defer with a real reason.** "I want to make sure I understand the full scope of the role and the comp structure before sharing a number — could you share the range for the role?"
2. **OK — give a wide range based on data.** "Based on what I've seen for similar new-grad roles, I'd expect total comp in the [X–Y] range, but I'm flexible based on the full package."
3. **Worst — give a single specific number.** Anchors low. Avoid.

If they push hard for a number, give a range tilted toward the top of what you've seen, framed as research-backed: "Based on Levels and conversations with people in similar roles, I've seen total comp in the [low end of high]–[high end of high] range. I'd want to learn more before getting more specific."

## When you receive the offer

Reactions matter. Don't accept on the call.

> "Thank you so much — I'm really excited. I want to make sure I give this the consideration it deserves. Could I take a few days to review and get back to you with any questions?"

This is standard and recruiters expect it. 3–5 business days is normal. Confirm in writing that you'll have until [specific date].

Then do three things:

1. **Get the offer in writing** with all components: base, sign-on, equity (vesting schedule, strike price if applicable), benefits start date, relocation.
2. **Calculate full first-year comp.** Don't compare base-to-base across offers — compare apples-to-apples on year 1 cash + vested equity.
3. **List your concerns.** What feels low? What feels missing? What would make you more excited?

## Making the ask

Negotiate over email when possible — gives you time to phrase, gives them time to socialize internally. Phone is fine if they prefer it.

### The structure

1. **Reaffirm enthusiasm.** "I'm really excited about the role and the team."
2. **State the ask clearly.** Specific number(s). Don't be vague.
3. **Give a justification.** Market data, competing context (real or directional), or specific value you bring.
4. **Leave room for them to respond.** Don't issue an ultimatum.

### Sample script — single offer, market-based

> Hi [Recruiter],
>
> Thanks again for the offer — I'm excited about the role.
>
> Before I sign, I wanted to ask about a couple of pieces. Based on what I've seen for similar roles (Levels.fyi, conversations with people in the field), the base of $X is on the lower end of the range. Is there flexibility to move it to $Y? And would a sign-on of $Z be possible — it would help with [relocation / etc.].
>
> [Company] is where I want to be — I'd like to land at a number where I can sign without second-guessing it. Happy to jump on a call if easier.
>
> Best,
> [Name]

Keep it short. Most successful negotiation emails are 4–6 sentences. Long, polished, performatively-earnest emails read as templated.

### Sample script — multiple offers (use carefully)

> Hi [Recruiter],
>
> Thank you for the offer — I'm genuinely excited and [Company] is at the top of my list.
>
> I've been transparent about being in conversations elsewhere, and I now have an offer from [other company / "another company in the space" if you want to be vague] at total comp of $X (base $A, sign-on $B, equity $C). I'd much rather be at [your company], and the gap is in the [base / sign-on] component.
>
> Is there flexibility to bring [specific component] closer to that level? I'd be ready to sign by [date] if we can get there.
>
> Best,
> [Name]

**Only do this with real offers.** Lying about competing offers is high-risk and small companies often check.

### Sample script — soft pushback when they say "no flexibility"

> I appreciate you checking. I understand base may be tight at this level. Would there be flexibility on the sign-on or relocation budget instead? Even a smaller adjustment there would help me get to yes.

Recruiters often have less flex on base (it's tied to bands and future raises) but more on one-time components.

## What to negotiate beyond base

For new-grad offers, in rough order of negotiability:

1. **Sign-on bonus.** Often the easiest lever. One-time cost to the company.
2. **Equity / RSUs / stock options** (where applicable).
3. **Relocation budget.** Even if you don't need it all, the cash equivalent helps.
4. **Start date.** Useful if you want time off post-graduation, or to align with a competing offer.
5. **PTO** (in some industries — less negotiable in big-company structured roles).
6. **Base salary.** Hardest at the new-grad level due to bands.
7. **Title / level.** Rare for new-grads but occasionally possible (e.g., L3 vs L4).

If you're given equity, understand:

- **Vesting schedule.** Standard is 4 years with a 1-year cliff. Some companies do 25/25/25/25, some front-load (e.g., 33/33/22/12 at Amazon).
- **Strike price** (for options) or **grant value** (for RSUs).
- **Refresh grants.** Do they give annual refreshers? Year 5 cliff is real if not.

## Exploding offers

Some companies issue "respond by Friday" offers. Push back:

> "I want to give this the consideration it deserves. Could I have until [date 7–10 days out] to make a decision? I'm in late stages with a few other companies and want to make a fully-informed choice."

Most recruiters will extend. If they refuse and the deadline is unreasonable (≤72 hours), that itself is data about how the company treats people.

## When to stop pushing

Negotiate firmly once. Maybe twice. Three rounds is usually too many — you risk burning goodwill before you've started. Read the recruiter's tone. If they say "I went back and got X — that's our final," it usually is final.

End every negotiation with a clear close:

> "Thank you for working with me on this. I'm signing today / by [date]. Looking forward to joining the team."

## After you sign

Be gracious. You'll work with these people. Send a thank-you note to the recruiter and the hiring manager. Don't rub the negotiation in anyone's face. Quietly enjoy the bump.

## Equity literacy for new grads

Equity is the part of the offer most new grads handwave through, which is how people end up with surprise tax bills or worthless paper. You don't need to be an expert. You need to understand the shape well enough to ask the right questions and know when to call a tax professional. Anything tax-specific below: talk to a CPA before you make a decision. This is orientation, not advice.

### RSUs vs. options

- **RSUs (Restricted Stock Units).** A promise that on a future date, the company will give you shares (or cash equivalent). You don't pay anything to receive them. When they vest, they're income — taxed at your ordinary income rate. Most large public companies grant RSUs.
- **Stock options.** The right (not obligation) to buy shares at a fixed "strike price" later. You only make money if the stock is worth more than the strike. Options come in two flavors:
  - **ISOs (Incentive Stock Options).** Reserved for employees, potentially better tax treatment, but trigger AMT (more on this below).
  - **NSOs (Non-Qualified Stock Options).** More flexible, simpler, but the spread between strike and fair market value at exercise is taxed as ordinary income immediately.

Rule of thumb: public-company new-grad offers are almost always RSUs. Early-stage startup offers are almost always options (usually ISOs).

### What 4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff actually means

Worked example. You're granted 4,000 RSUs, 4-year vest, 1-year cliff, vesting monthly after the cliff.

- **Months 1–11:** zero vested. If you leave, you walk away with nothing.
- **Month 12 (the "cliff"):** 1,000 RSUs vest in a single chunk (25% of grant).
- **Months 13–48:** the remaining 3,000 vest monthly, ~83 per month.
- **Month 48:** all 4,000 vested.

Leaving at month 11 means zero. Leaving at month 13 means 1,083. The cliff is real and shapes job-hopping decisions.

### 83(b) elections

Relevant if you receive **restricted stock** (not RSUs) or **early-exercise options** at a startup. An 83(b) lets you pay tax now on the (usually tiny) value at grant, instead of later as it vests at presumably much higher values. You have **30 days from grant** to file — miss it and the option is gone forever.

For new grads at public companies receiving standard RSUs: 83(b) does not apply. For new grads at early-stage startups with early-exercise provisions: it might matter a lot. Talk to a tax professional within the first two weeks of starting.

### Public-company RSU taxation (single-trigger)

When public-company RSUs vest, the value is treated as ordinary income on the day of vesting. Most companies sell-to-cover — they sell ~22–37% of the vesting shares to cover withholding. If your actual marginal rate is higher than the default withholding rate, you can owe more in April. Set aside cash or talk to a CPA.

### Private-company traps

Private-company RSUs and options are where new grads get hurt:

- **Private-company RSUs** sometimes have **double-trigger** vesting (see below) — you don't owe tax until both time-vesting and a liquidity event happen. Cleaner.
- **ISOs at a private company** can trigger **AMT (Alternative Minimum Tax)** when you exercise, even though you haven't sold and have no cash. The "spread" between strike price and current 409A valuation counts for AMT purposes. People have owed five- and six-figure tax bills on illiquid paper. Before exercising any ISOs at a private company, talk to a tax professional. This is the single most expensive mistake in this whole document.

### Single-trigger vs. double-trigger acceleration

These terms appear in offer letters and matter at acquisition or IPO:

- **Single-trigger acceleration.** A single event (usually acquisition) accelerates some or all of your unvested equity. Rare for new grads.
- **Double-trigger acceleration.** Two events required — typically acquisition *and* you being terminated without cause within some window after. Standard protection for employees so the acquirer can't fire you the day after closing to claw back your equity.

For new grads: you usually don't have negotiating power on these clauses, but read your offer to know what you have.

### The one-line summary

Understand vesting, ask whether RSUs are single- or double-trigger if you're at a private company, never exercise private-company ISOs without talking to a CPA, and remember that a "$200K equity grant" over 4 years is $50K/year of pre-tax, often illiquid value — not a check.

## Things to never do

- **Lie about competing offers.** Smaller industries find out. It's catastrophic.
- **Negotiate by threat.** "If you don't give me X, I'll take the other offer." Read as immature.
- **Negotiate after you've already verbally accepted.** That's reneging, not negotiating.
- **Forget to negotiate because you're flattered.** The sting of leaving $10K+ on the table fades slower than the flattery.
