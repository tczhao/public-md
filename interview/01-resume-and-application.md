# 01 — Resume and Application

Your resume's only job is to get you a 30-minute recruiter call. It is not your autobiography, not a portfolio, not a list of every class you took. Optimize relentlessly for the 6-second skim.

## The 6-second skim test

You don't need a friend for this. Open your resume on your phone, look at it for 6 seconds, then close it. Ask yourself:

- What role does this say I'm targeting?
- What's the most impressive thing above the fold?
- What's the second most impressive thing?

If you can't answer all three from memory, the resume is broken. Recruiters skim on phones too. Fix layout and bullet hierarchy until the answers fall out instantly.

Bonus check: if you have a friend, repeat the test with them after you've fixed it. Outsiders catch what you don't.

## Format rules (non-negotiable)

- **One page.** Two pages for new grads signals "I don't know what to cut."
- **PDF, not Word.** Naming: `FirstName_LastName_Role_Company.pdf`.
- **Single column, plain serif or sans-serif.** No headshots, no rainbow colors, no skill bars, no "languages: English ▮▮▮▮▯".
- **Consistent tense and punctuation.** Past roles past tense, current roles present tense. Either all bullets end with periods or none do.
- **ATS-readable.** No tables, no text boxes, no graphics. The parser strips them and your resume becomes garbage.

## Section order for new grads

1. **Header** — name, phone, email, LinkedIn URL, GitHub/portfolio URL, city. No street address.
2. **Education** — school, degree, major, graduation date, GPA if ≥3.5. Honors, scholarships, relevant coursework only if it adds signal.
3. **Experience** — internships, part-time jobs, research. Reverse chronological.
4. **Projects** — only the 2–3 best. School projects are fine if they're substantial.
5. **Skills** — concise, grouped (Languages / Tools / Other). No "Microsoft Word."
6. *(Optional)* Leadership / Activities — only if it's genuinely impressive or fills space tied to the role.

## The bullet formula

Every bullet should follow the structure:

> **[Strong verb] [what you did] [how / with what tools] [quantified result or scope]**

Examples — bad → good:

- "Worked on a data dashboard in Tableau."
  → "Built a Tableau dashboard tracking 12 KPIs across 4 regions, adopted by 30+ sales reps and cited in weekly leadership review."
- "Helped with marketing analysis."
  → "Analyzed 18 months of campaign data in SQL and Excel, identified $40K in wasted spend, recommended reallocation adopted in next quarter's plan."
- "Was president of the data club."
  → "Led 5-person exec team running a 120-member analytics club; grew attendance 60% YoY by launching a peer-led case competition."

Quantify everything you can. If you can't quantify outcome, quantify scope (people, hours, lines of code, dollars handled, rows of data, transactions).

## Verbs that pull weight

`Built, designed, launched, led, owned, automated, reduced, increased, shipped, prototyped, presented, analyzed, modeled, deployed, mentored, scoped, negotiated, redesigned`.

Verbs that don't: `helped, assisted, participated, worked on, was responsible for, contributed to`. These describe presence, not impact.

## Tailoring (the cheap version)

For each application:

1. Open the JD. Highlight the 5–8 most repeated nouns and verbs.
2. Make sure those words appear in your resume where true.
3. Reorder bullets so the most JD-relevant one is first under each role.
4. Adjust the project section to feature the most JD-relevant project.

This takes 10–15 minutes per application and roughly doubles callback rate vs. a generic resume.

## The cover letter

Most companies don't read them. The ones that do are usually a positive selection — smaller orgs, mission-driven roles, hiring managers who care. Default to having one ready.

Format: 3 short paragraphs, ~250 words.

1. **Hook + why this company specifically.** One sentence that proves you read more than the JD. Reference a product decision, a recent post, a customer they serve. Do not say "I am writing to apply for…"
2. **Why you for this role.** Pick the 2 most relevant things on your resume and connect them to what the role needs. Don't restate the resume.
3. **Close.** One sentence on what you'd bring + a clear ask for a conversation.

## Where applications actually come from

Rough breakdown of where new-grad offers originate:

- Referrals: 30–50%
- Career fairs / on-campus recruiting: 15–25%
- Cold applications via company website: 15–25%
- LinkedIn Easy Apply / job boards: 5–15%
- Recruiter outreach: 5–10%

Allocate effort accordingly. Spending 20 hours a week on LinkedIn Easy Apply is a top mistake.

## Application hygiene

- **Apply within 72 hours** of a posting going live. Many roles fill from the first ~50 applicants.
- **One company at a time.** Don't apply to 5 roles at the same company simultaneously — recruiters dedupe and it looks scattershot.
- **Use the company portal**, not just LinkedIn. The data flows are cleaner.
- **Set up alerts** on LinkedIn and the 5–10 companies you care most about.
- **Track everything** in your sheet. The "did I apply here already?" question gets old fast.

## Sample bullets by experience type

Most new grads don't lack experience — they lack the discipline to describe it well. The same activity can read as filler or as proof. Below, weak (W) → strong (S) for the four buckets new grads actually have.

### Class projects

- W: "Worked on a group project predicting housing prices."
  S: "Built a gradient-boosted regression model on 21K Zillow records (Python, scikit-learn); cut MAE 18% vs. linear baseline and presented results to a 40-person class."
- W: "Did a database project for my CS class."
  S: "Designed a normalized 14-table Postgres schema for a mock airline reservation system; wrote 30+ queries with indexes that reduced average query time from 2.1s to 80ms."
- W: "Capstone project on customer segmentation."
  S: "Segmented 12K e-commerce customers using K-means and RFM analysis in Python; surfaced 3 segments worth ~62% of revenue and recommended targeted retention plays adopted by the partner company's marketing team."
- W: "Wrote a research paper on misinformation."
  S: "Coded 1,200 tweets across 3 misinformation topics; ran inter-rater reliability (Cohen's κ = 0.81) and co-authored a 12-page paper accepted to the department's undergraduate research symposium."

### Part-time / retail / service jobs

Don't hide these. Quantify the operational reality.

- W: "Cashier at Trader Joe's."
  S: "Handled 200+ customer transactions per shift in a high-volume Trader Joe's store; trained 4 new hires on POS workflow and store layout, cutting their ramp-up from 2 weeks to 5 days."
- W: "Worked as a barista."
  S: "Ran morning rush solo at a 60-cover café; managed inventory for 14 SKUs and reduced milk waste 22% by retooling the par-level sheet."
- W: "Server at a restaurant."
  S: "Served 30+ tables per shift in a Friday-night fine-dining rotation; consistently top-3 in upsell revenue out of 18 servers and trained the next two new hires."
- W: "Tutored students in math."
  S: "Tutored 9 high-school students in AP Calculus over 2 semesters; 8 of 9 scored 4 or 5 on the AP exam, a result I tracked and followed up on."

### Club leadership

- W: "President of the Investment Club."
  S: "Led 6-person exec team for a 90-member investment club; relaunched the stock-pitch competition after a 2-year hiatus, drawing 11 team submissions and 3 alumni judges."
- W: "Member of Society of Women Engineers."
  S: "Coordinated SWE's fall mentorship program; matched 48 underclassmen with 24 industry mentors and ran 3 panel events averaging 70 attendees each."
- W: "Treasurer of the Data Science Club."
  S: "Managed $8K annual budget for a 130-member data science club; renegotiated the cloud-credits sponsorship with AWS Educate, tripling per-member compute access at no added cost."
- W: "Helped organize hackathon."
  S: "Co-led logistics for a 250-participant 24-hour hackathon; secured 4 corporate sponsors ($14K total), recruited 12 judges, and ran the day-of ops desk."

### Personal side projects

- W: "Made a Twitter sentiment analysis tool."
  S: "Built and deployed a Streamlit app that scores Twitter sentiment on user-supplied keywords (Python, Tweepy, VADER); 200+ unique users in the first month, source on GitHub."
- W: "Built a personal finance tracker."
  S: "Shipped a personal-finance tracker (Next.js + Supabase) that auto-categorizes Plaid transactions; live at [url], 30+ active users from r/personalfinance, 4.6-star feedback in the launch thread."
- W: "Made a Discord bot."
  S: "Wrote a Python Discord bot that pulls live Valorant match stats for a 1.2K-member server; processes ~400 commands/day and has run without intervention for 9 months."
- W: "Started a blog about machine learning."
  S: "Publish a fortnightly Substack breaking down recent ML papers in plain English; 800 subscribers in 5 months, two posts shared by paper authors on Twitter."

If you genuinely cannot quantify, name the artifact: a public URL, a repo, a PDF, a reproducible benchmark. Recruiters trust things they can click.

## Red flags to fix before sending

- Typos. Especially in the company name. Auto-rejection at most places.
- Unprofessional email (`xXskaterboiXx@…`). Use `firstname.lastname@`.
- LinkedIn URL says `linkedin.com/in/firstnamelastname-238472399`. Customize it.
- "References available upon request." Wastes a line. Cut.
- Objective statement at the top. Wastes 5 lines. Cut.
- Bullets longer than 2 lines. Tighten.
- A "Skills" section listing every tool you've heard of. Cut to ~12 you can actually answer questions on.
